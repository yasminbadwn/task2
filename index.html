Space

Outer space is the vacuum present between celestial bodies, including planet Earth. It is not completely empty but consists of a relative vacuum with a low density of particles, primarily hydrogen and helium plasma, as well as electromagnetic radiation, magnetic fields, and neutrons. Recent observations have also shown that it contains dark matter and dark energy. The baseline temperature, determined by the residual radiation from the Big Bang, is 2.7 Kelvin. The extremely low-density plasma (less than one hydrogen atom per cubic meter) and the high temperature (millions of Kelvin) in intergalactic space are considered mostly in terms of ordinary baryonic matter in outer space; local concentrations are condensed into stars and galaxies. Intergalactic space occupies a larger volume of the universe, and even galaxies and stellar systems are mostly empty space, with planets occupying nearly the vacant space.

Planets of the Solar System

In 2006, the International Astronomical Union (IAU) provided the most recent definition of a planet. According to this definition, a planet is a celestial body that is spherical in shape, has sufficient gravity to maintain its shape, and orbits a star, such as the Sun, in a specific orbit. A planet is also characterized by its large size. There are currently 8 planets in the solar system, and here are the names of the planets in order of their distance from the Sun

  • Mercury
  • Venus
  • Earth
  • Mars
  • Jupiter
  • Saturn
  • Uranus
  • Neptune

Nasa

NASA stands for the National Aeronautics and Space Administration. It is the United States government agency responsible for the nation's civilian space program and for aeronautics and aerospace research.

Key Facts About NASA:
Founded: NASA was established on July 29, 1958, and began operations on October 1, 1958.
Headquarters: NASA's headquarters is located in Washington, D.C.
Missions: NASA is known for its space exploration missions, including the Apollo Moon-landing missions, the Mars rovers, and the Hubble Space Telescope.
